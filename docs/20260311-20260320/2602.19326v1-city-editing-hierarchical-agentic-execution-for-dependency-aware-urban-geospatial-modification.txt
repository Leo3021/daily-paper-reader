# Abstract 

As cities evolve over time, challenges such as traf-fic congestion and functional imbalance increas-ingly necessitate urban renewal through efficient modification of existing plans, rather than com-plete re-planning. In practice, even minor urban changes require substantial manual effort to re-draw geospatial layouts, slowing the iterative plan-ning and decision-making procedure. Motivated by recent advances in agentic systems and multi-modal reasoning, we formulate urban renewal as a machine-executable task that iteratively modi-fies existing urban plans represented in structured geospatial formats. More specifically, we repre-sent urban layouts using GeoJSON and decom-pose natural-language editing instructions into hierarchical geometric intents spanning polygon-, line-, and point-level operations. To coordinate in-terdependent edits across spatial elements and ab-straction levels, we propose a hierarchical agentic framework that jointly performs multi-level plan-ning and execution with explicit propagation of intermediate spatial constraints. We further intro-duce an iterative execution–validation mechanism that mitigates error accumulation and enforces global spatial consistency during multi-step edit-ing. Extensive experiments across diverse urban editing scenarios demonstrate significant improve-ments in efficiency, robustness, correctness, and spatial validity over existing baselines. Our code and dataset are publicly accessible 1.

# 1. Introduction 

As urban environments evolve, cities increasingly exhibit localized deficiencies such as traffic congestion, functional imbalance, and inefficient land use. Addressing these is-sues rarely involves redesigning urban environments from scratch. Instead, contemporary urban renewal proceeds through targeted, iterative modification of existing urban plans. Planners incrementally revise specific regions, func-tions, or infrastructures while preserving global spatial con-sistency. Figure 1 contrasts this emerging renewal workflow with traditional urban planning, highlighting a paradigm shift from from-scratch plan generation to incremental re-finement of existing urban environments .In practice, urban renewal is a highly deliberative and iter-ative process involving repeated coordination among plan-ners, stakeholders, and regulatory bodies. To evaluate al-ternatives and justify proposed changes, planners must re-peatedly revise existing urban layouts, often performing fine-grained edits over complex geospatial structures. Even minor modifications can require substantial manual effort to ensure geometric validity, dependency consistency, and compliance with planning constraints. For example, expand-ing a park boundary may require adjusting nearby paths and relocating adjacent street furniture to preserve connectiv-ity and buffer constraints. Consequently, current urban re-newal workflows remain slow, labor-intensive, and difficult to scale. This limitation highlights the need for computa-tional support to reliably assist iterative decision-making over existing urban plans. Despite recent advances in urban intelligence, prior compu-tational approaches largely fall short of this requirement. Existing methods can be broadly categorized into three paradigms: 1) Generative AI–based methods learn distri-butions over urban layouts and synthesize complete plans under given conditions (Liu et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2026). While effective at capturing global spatial patterns, they assume that planning objectives can be achieved through one-shot generation. 2) Optimization-based methods for-mulate planning as a sequential decision-making problem with predefined objectives and constrained action spaces, often relying on simplified abstractions of planning opera-1

Despite their methodological differences, these paradigms share a fundamental limitation: they treat urban planning as a holistic generation problem, rather than supporting the iterative, dependency-aware modification processes that characterize real-world urban renewal. To bridge this gap, we study how urban renewal can be supported as an automated process that operates directly on existing urban plans. Our objective is to assist planners by enabling efficient, reliable, and incremental refinement of current urban environments. This perspective calls for a computational framework capable of converting high-level renewal intents into executable actions and applying them it-eratively while preserving spatial constraints. However, real-izing such a framework poses three key research challenges. 

Challenge 1: Executable Editing over Existing Plans. 

Urban renewal must be formalized as a machine-executable task defined over existing urban layouts. This requires a clear specification of inputs, permissible editing operations, and outputs that enable automated execution on structured geospatial representations. Challenge 2: Dependency- and Constraint-Aware Iteration. Iterative modifications in-troduce complex dependencies across urban entities and geometric levels. Effectively addressing these dependencies requires explicit reasoning mechanisms to propagate con-straints across editing stages and maintain global structural consistency. Challenge 3: Intermediate Error Detection and Correction. Iterative execution inevitably introduces intermediate errors, particularly when edits interact across multiple spatial elements. Without systematic detection and correction, such errors can accumulate and compromise the validity of the final urban plan. To address these challenges, we formulate urban renewal as a city geospatial editing problem over existing urban plans represented in GeoJSON format, and propose a hierar-chical agentic execution framework, named CEAE . At its core, CEAE treats urban renewal as a structured execution process with cross-level dependencies and failure-sensitive intermediate states. It adopts a geometry-aware, coarse-to-fine editing strategy across polygon-, line-, and point-level representations, enabling structured and dependency-aware plan modification. Editing intents are decomposed and assigned to geometry-specific agents, each invoking level-dependent geospatial tools to perform targeted edits. Intermediate results produced at higher geometric levels are explicitly propagated to downstream agents, allowing subsequent edits to condition on upstream decisions and spatial constraints. To ensure execution reliability, CEAE integrates a self-reflective validation module after each ex-ecution subtask to verify geometric validity, dependency consistency, and constraint satisfaction. Only validated in-termediate states are committed and aggregated to produce the final updated GeoJSON plan, together with auxiliary execution traces and summaries. In summary, our contributions are threefold: • Problem. We introduce city geospatial editing as a structured computational paradigm that reframes urban renewal as dependency-aware layout modification with iterative error correction mechanisms. • Algorithm. We propose a hierarchical agentic exe-cution framework that performs multi-level geometric editing via structured dependency propagation and self-reflective validation strategies. • Evaluation. We conduct large-scale empirical eval-uation that demonstrates consistent gains in editing correctness, structural validity, and robustness across diverse real-world urban renewal scenarios.

# 3. Methodology 

3.1. Framework Overview 

Figure 2 illustrates the overall architecture of CEAE, which includes four key components: 1) task planner agent; 2) geoexecutor agent; 3) validator agent; 4) aggregator agent. Given an existing urban plan in GeoJSON format and a natural-language editing instruction, CEAE performs urban geospatial editing through a staged agentic workflow. The task planner agent first interprets the instruction and gen-erates a structured editing plan comprising a sequence of geometry-level editing intents. These intents are executed in a coarse-to-fine manner across different geometric abstrac-tion levels. At each stage, a geometry-specific geoexecutor agent performs targeted edits by invoking level-dependent tools, while intermediate outputs produced at coarser levels are propagated as contextual inputs to subsequent finer-grained stages. To ensure execution reliability, each geoex-ecutor agent is paired with a validator agent that verifies intermediate results in terms of geometric validity, depen-dency consistency, and constraint compliance. After all stages are completed, the aggregator agent integrates all val-idated edits to generate the final updated plan, together with a concise execution summary and detailed editing records. 

3.2. Task Planning and Editing Intent Decomposition 

Why planning and decomposition matters. Urban renewal requests are typically expressed as high-level, fragmented natural-language instructions, while their execution requires coordinated, fine-grained modifications over heterogeneous spatial elements. Such requests implicitly involve multiple objectives, spatial constraints, and interdependent opera-tions across geometric abstraction levels. Direct execution without explicit decomposition obscures these dependencies and often leads to invalid intermediate states. To address this structural mismatch, we introduce a task planning mech-anism that transforms high-level instructions into structured, machine-executable editing intents. 

Task Planner Fplan : The task planner operates at the plan-ning level and is responsible for reasoning over natural-language editing instructions. By explicitly organizing editing intents across geometric levels, the planner enables reliable coarse-to-fine execution, where higher-level modifi-cations provide valid contextual constraints for subsequent finer-grained edits. 3.4. Self-Reflective Validation and Result Aggregation 

Why self-reflective validation matters. As intermediate geospatial states propagate across execution stages, locally valid edits may still produce globally invalid layouts, violat-ing geometric, dependency, or intent-level constraints. Once introduced, such invalid states contaminate subsequent edits and lead to error accumulation. To prevent invalid state propagation, we incorporate self-reflective validation into the execution loop as an explicit control signal that governs whether intermediate states are accepted and propagated. 

Validator Fval : Given the intermediate execution outcomes produced during geoexecution, self-reflective validation is applied to perform subtask-level admissibility. Throughout the execution process, validation acts as a gating mecha-nism that determines whether intermediate outcomes can be propagated to subsequent execution. This self-reflective validation paradigm ensures that only validated interme-diate outcomes condition later execution, preventing the propagation of invalid results. 

Execution-Level Validation and Re-Execution Control. 

The validator operates as an execution-level control module tightly coupled with subtask execution. After each subtask, the validator evaluates the execution outcome with respect to the current geospatial state and the corresponding subtask intent. Validated outcomes are admitted and execution proceeds, while invalid outcomes trigger re-execution with structured diagnostic feedback. To ensure execution effi-ciency, we impose a global maximum limit of re-execution attempts across all subtasks. We formalize self-reflective validation as an execution-level admissibility decision process. Let Iℓ denotes the set of edit-ing intents at level ℓ and Tℓ = {τℓ, 1, . . . , τ ℓ,K ℓ } denotes the corresponding sequence of subtasks. Validation is modeled as an admissibility checking process interleaved with execu-tion. Given an execution outcome (Oℓ,k , G ℓ,k +1 ) produced by subtask τℓ,k under the geospatial state Gℓ,k , the validator returns a validation decision: 

Vℓ,k = Fval (Iℓ, τ ℓ,k , G ℓ,k , O ℓ,k , G ℓ,k +1 ) , (5) where Vℓ,k ∈ { accept , reject } indicates whether the execu-tion outcome is admissible. The validation procedure in Equation 5 applies subtask-specific criteria. For informa-tional subtasks, the validator checks consistency between the reported observation, the current geospatial state, and the querying intent. For state-updating subtasks, it verifies geometric validity and consistency with the editing intent and previously validated states. If Vℓ,k is accept , the exe-cution outcome is admitted and propagated to subsequent execution. Otherwise, the execution outcome is discarded and the subtask is re-executed. By admitting only validated intermediate outcomes, self-reflective validation localizes execution errors and prevents their propagation, preserving global validity throughout hierarchical execution.

Only validated state-updating results are committed to the final geospatial state, while execution outcomes of all subtasks are recorded for traceability. In addition to generating the final updated GeoJSON plan, the aggregator consolidates subtask-level execution traces, validation decisions, re-execution statistics, and auxiliary metadata for final reporting or traceability. Overall, these results demonstrate the robustness of our framework under prompt-level perturbations.

A.1. Preliminaries 

A.1.1. G EO JSON For clarity and completeness, we briefly introduce GeoJSON, which serves as the underlying data representation throughout this work. Figure 7 provides an illustrative example of a GeoJSON file and its corresponding geometric visualization. GeoJSON is a widely used geospatial data format for representing geographic features and their associated attributes in a structured and machine-readable manner. It encodes spatial information using three fundamental geometry types: 

Point/Node , LineString , and Polygon , which correspond to discrete locations, linear structures, and areal regions, respectively. In GeoJSON, each urban geospatial element is represented as a Feature object that consists of a unique identifier, a geometry field, and a properties field. The geometry field specifies the spatial shape of the element through a set of coordinates defined under a common spatial reference system. The properties field stores non-spatial semantic attributes such as land-use type, functional category, or planning-related metadata. The identifier enables consistent referencing and tracking of elements during editing operations. These components provide a unified representation that supports both geometric manipulation and semantic reasoning. Accordingly, urban geospatial editing operations can be formulated as modifications to feature geometries, properties, or their induced spatial relationships. Thus, we adopt GeoJSON as the urban geospatial data representation. A.1.2. E DITING TASKS 

We define three levels of urban geospatial editing tasks: point editing, line editing, and polygon editing. These task levels naturally align with the three fundamental geometric features in GeoJSON. Level-1 point editing focuses on localized coordinate displacement, involving minimal geometric and topological constraints. Level-2 line editing focuses on the controlled extension or truncation of road-like linear features, introducing structural dependencies related to directionality and network connectivity. Level-3 polygon editing is the most challenging scenario, requiring area modifications to satisfy global semantic targets (e.g., target green ratios) while respecting complex geometric and topological constraints. Across all three levels, we employ the same agentic editing pipeline to the editing tasks. A.1.3. H UMAN INSTRUCTIONS 

In this work, human instructions specify the desired modifications to urban geospatial elements, serving as high-level semantic guidance for the editing process. They describe what should be changed and what outcome should be achieved, rather than prescribing the exact low-level operations or execution procedures. A.3. Experimental Setup and Reproducibility 

A.3.1. H YPERPARAMETERS AND REPRODUCIBILITY .We adopt a unified set of hyperparameters within each execution paradigm to ensure reproducibility and fair comparison. All language model inferences are conducted using deterministic decoding by default. For the task planner agent, different decoding configurations are used for different task levels. For Level-1 and Level-2 editing tasks, we use non-sampling decoding with do sample = False to encourage stable and conservative planning behavior. For the Level-3 editing task, we enable sampling and adopt a higher-temperature configuration with temperature = 0 .9 and top p = 0 .9 to allow greater planning flexibility. The maximum number of newly generated tokens is only increased when replanning is triggered due to detected plan-level semantic conflicts. In such cases, a larger max new tokens budget (set to 400 ) is used to allow the task planner to revise the task decomposition and resolve semantic inconsistencies. For the execution–validation process, we allow up to R = 3 corrective retries per sub-task to bound the overall computation. A.3.2. B ASELINE LANGUAGE MODELS 

We select a diverse set of closed-source and open-source large language models as baseline backbones for single-pass city editing. These models are chosen to span multiple model families and a wide range of parameter scales, providing a broad view of one-shot editing performance across different capacity regimes. Specifically, we include GPT-5-Mini and GPT-5-Nano as closed-source baselines, and evaluate open-source models including LLaMA-3-8B and LLaMA-3.1-8B (AI@Meta, 2024), Qwen2.5 models with 3B and 7B parameters (Team, 2024), Qwen3-8B (Team, 2025), and DeepSeek-R1 distillation variants built on 1.5B, 7B, 8B, and 14B backbones (DeepSeek-AI, 2025). The DeepSeek-R1 distillation models transfer the reasoning behaviors of the DeepSeek-R1 teacher into different backbone architectures, such as LLaMA and Qwen. Overall, these baselines cover a wide range of architectures and parameter sizes, ranging from lightweight 1.5B models to larger 14B models. For each backbone baseline, the full set of point, line, and polygon editing tasks is evaluated using the same dataset and evaluation metrics. A.3.3. E XPERIMENTAL SETTINGS 

We conduct all experiments on Ubuntu Linux operating system with kernel version 5.15.0. The system is equipped with an AMD Ryzen Threadripper PRO 7965WX CPU with 24 cores and 250 GB of system memory. For GPU acceleration, we use a single NVIDIA RTX 6000 Ada Generation GPU with 48 GB of memory. All agentic components and geospatial operations are implemented in Python 3.10.13. Model inference and execution are built on PyTorch (Paszke et al., 2019) with CUDA 12.4 support. Backbone language models are loaded and executed using the Hugging Face Transformers library (Wolf et al., 2020).
