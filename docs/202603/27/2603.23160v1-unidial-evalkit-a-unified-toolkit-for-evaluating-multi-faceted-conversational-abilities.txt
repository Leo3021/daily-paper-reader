Abstract 

Benchmarking AI systems in multi-turn inter-active scenarios is essential for understanding their practical capabilities in real-world appli-cations. However, existing evaluation proto-cols are highly heterogeneous, differing sig-nificantly in dataset formats, model interfaces, and evaluation pipelines, which severely im-pedes systematic comparison. In this work, we present UniDial-EvalKit (UDE) , a uni-fied evaluation toolkit for assessing interac-tive AI systems. The core contribution of UDE lies in its holistic unification: it stan-dardizes heterogeneous data formats into a universal schema, streamlines complex eval-uation pipelines through a modular architec-ture, and aligns metric calculations under a consistent scoring interface. It also supports efficient large-scale evaluation through parallel generation and scoring, as well as checkpoint-based caching to eliminate redundant computa-tion. Validated across diverse multi-turn bench-marks, UDE not only guarantees high repro-ducibility through standardized workflows and transparent logging, but also significantly im-proves evaluation efficiency and extensibility. We make the complete toolkit and evaluation scripts publicly available to foster a standard-ized benchmarking ecosystem and accelerate future breakthroughs in interactive AI. 

1 Introduction 

In the rapidly evolving landscape of large language models (LLMs) and agent-based systems, assess-ing their performance in multi-turn interactive dia-logues has become essential for understanding their practical capabilities. However, evaluating these systems remains a complex challenge due to a lack of unified standards and frameworks for dialogue assessment. This fragmentation complicates the comparison of models’ performances and limits the ability to track improvements across different tasks and scenarios. While evaluation frameworks for single-turn sce-narios, such as OpenCompass (Contributors, 2023) and VLMEvalKit (Duan et al., 2024), are well-established, the structural unification of multi-turn evaluations remains largely underexplored. Unlike isolated single-turn interactions, assessing contin-uous multi-turn systems introduces unique com-plexities, primarily presenting the following three challenges: First, multi-turn evaluation data lacks a unified schema due to highly inconsistent assessment de-signs. To capture a system’s true capabilities in continuous interactions, different benchmarks have tailored their own evaluation scopes, such as judg-ing only the final response (Deshpande et al., 2025; Wu et al., 2024; Hu et al., 2025), assessing every in-dividual turn (Bai et al., 2024; He et al., 2024; Yan et al., 2025), or some specific turns (Kwan et al., 2024; Jiayang et al., 2026). This lack of structural consensus impedes the unification of evaluation frameworks across different setups. Second, inconsistencies in evaluators and scor-ing metrics further fragment the assessment land-scape. Many benchmarks adopt automated evalua-tors, such as LLMs-as-judges (Zheng et al., 2023; Li et al., 2025), but they rely on heterogeneous base models and scoring frameworks. For example, Fan et al. (2025) adopted GPT-4 as a judge while He et al. (2024) chose GPT-4o and Claude. The absence of unified workflow hinders the seamless application of advancing evaluation metrics across benchmarks. Third, the lack of standardized score aggregation strategies further fragments multi-turn evaluation. Benchmarks employ diverse methods to summarize turn-level metrics, such as computing the mean (He et al., 2024), minimum (Bai et al., 2024; Cao et al., 2026), or maximum across turns, resulting in over-all scores that often reflect the aggregation heuris-tic rather than the model’s true capabilities. To address these challenges, we present UniDial-EvalKit (UDE), a unified evaluation framework for multi-turn conversational systems, including both LLMs and agent-based models. UDE is designed to systematically standardize the evaluation pro-cess by decoupling dataset management, model interaction, and metric computation into modular components, enabling flexible integration and fair comparison across diverse benchmarks. It provides a consistent dialogue evaluation schema, aligned metrics across heterogeneous judge models, and unified score aggregation. Beyond standardization, UDE also supports large-scale parallel processing and checkpoint-based resume functionality, improving both effi-ciency and scalability. The framework includes a comprehensive suite of benchmarks covering mem-ory, safety, instruction-following, and etc., offering holistic assessment of conversational capabilities. Its modular design also allows users to incorporate new models and metrics while maintaining unified evaluation standards across all tests, addressing the fragmentation and inconsistencies identified in existing multi-turn evaluation practices. UDE is publicly available under the Apache 2.0 License. The repository includes the complete source code, installation instructions, and documen-tation for configuring and running evaluations. In the following sections, we will explain the design and features of UniDial-EvalKit, analyze results from various models, and discuss future directions for extending the framework’s capabilities. 

2 UniDial-EvalKit Design 

UniDial-EvalKit (UDE) is conceptualized as a modular, unified framework tailored for evaluat-ing the multi-turn conversational capabilities of LLMs and Agents. To maximize extensibility and maintainability, the framework architecture decou-ples the evaluation pipeline into four sequential and independent phases: Data Loading ( DataPhase ), Model Generation ( GenerationPhase ), Metric Eval-uation ( EvaluationPhase ), and Result Aggregation (AggregationPhase ). This highly modular design enables researchers to seamlessly integrate novel datasets, custom models, or specialized evaluation metrics without modifying the core pipeline. Fur-thermore, UDE supports both a Command-Line Interface (CLI) and a comprehensive Python API (EvalPipeline ), offering flexible deployment op-tions across diverse experimental setups. 

2.1 Unified Schema 

To homogenize the disparate and complex formats of various multi-turn dialogue benchmarks, UDE introduces a standardized, centralized data schema anchored by the Dialog object as follows: 

Dialog = { "dialog_id": ..., "dialog_raw_info": {...}, "dialog_labels": {...}, "dialog_eval_config": { "use_reference_history": ... }, "dialog_turns": [ {"turn_id": ..., "role": ..., "content": ..., "reference": ..., "reference_document": ..., "eval_config": { "do_eval": ..., "metrics": [ {"class_name": ..., "args": {...} }, ... ], }, "turn_labels": {...} }, ... ]}

During the data loading stage, all raw data is parsed into a list of normalized in-stances. Each Dialog encapsulates session-level metadata (e.g., dialog_id , dialog_labels )and a chronological sequence of interactions (dialog_turns ). Each turn records its role 

(system, user, or assistant), textual content, reference answers, and fine-grained evaluation con-figuration ( eval_config ). At the dialogue level, 

dialog_eval_config.use_reference_history 

determines whether the generation of subsequent turns follows an on-policy strategy based on previous outputs. At the turn level, eval_config 

specifies whether the turn should be evaluated and which one or more metrics to apply. This 2unified schema cleanly separates dialogue-level and turn-level labels and evaluation settings, abstracting away dataset-specific structures and enabling downstream generation and evaluation modules to operate in a fully agnostic manner. 

2.2 Model & Agent Integration 

To support both open-source and proprietary mod-els, UDE provides a standardized BaseModel in-terface. Built-in wrappers enable seamless eval-uation of OpenAI-compatible APIs and local models deployed via inference engines such as vLLM. For stateful agent systems, the generation pipeline includes explicit lifecycle management (begin_dialog and end_dialog ), allowing agents to maintain coherent, dynamically updated con-textual memory during multi-turn sessions. This design ensures that internal states and caches are properly cleared after each dialogue, preventing cross-dialogue context contamination. 

2.3 Evaluation Methodology 

UDE adopts a flexible, metric-agnostic evaluation paradigm. During the evaluation phase, the frame-work dynamically loads and executes evaluation metrics based on the specific configurations de-fined within each assistant Turn . All supported metrics are managed via a centralized registry (METRIC_REGISTRY ), which currently encompasses a diverse taxonomy of evaluation protocols includ-ing LLM-as-a-Judge, Exact Match, Instruction Ad-herence Accuracy, and task-specific metrics for mathematics and coding. The evaluation module calculates scores by contextualizing the model’s prediction against reference answers and the pre-ceding dialogue history, subsequently persisting the scores and turn-level metadata as structured records to facilitate granular capability analysis. 

2.4 Score Aggregation 

To synthesize granular turn-level evaluations into macroscopic capability profiles, UDE implements a hierarchical aggregation strategy. During the Ag-gregationPhase , users can flexibly configure statis-tical pooling algorithms across three layers: • Turn-level: Multiple metric scores within a sin-gle interaction are combined to produce a turn-level summary, for example via mean, minimum, or maximum aggregation. • Dialog-level: Turn-level summaries are further aggregated across a session to yield a dialogue-level score. Minimum aggregation is set as the default strategy where a single failure can com-promise the entire dialogue. • Dataset-level: Dialogue-level scores are aggre-gated across the dataset to produce the final per-formance measure, either by averaging session scores or by flattening all turns globally. Additionally, the framework supports independent aggregation partitioned by specific metric names (agg_by_metric ), providing researchers with de-tailed analytical insights into distinct dimensions of model performance. 

2.5 Efficiency & Acceleration 

Given the substantial computational overhead and latency inherent in evaluating multi-turn interac-tions, UDE is engineered with multithreading and fault-tolerance mechanisms. During both the gen-eration and evaluation phases, the framework lever-ages ThreadPoolExecutor to facilitate efficient, dialogue-level concurrent processing. To mitigate network instability or unexpected interruptions, UDE features an automatic resumption capabil-ity. It scans the output directory to identify the latest checkpoints and processes only the pending dialogues. This checkpoint-based mechanism miti-gates redundant computation, ensuring stable and efficient large-scale evaluation.

3.2 Evaluated Models and Agents 

We include a diverse set of models and agents, with the suite continuously expanding. 

Models. The preliminary model set consists of DeepSeek-V3.2 (Liu et al., 2025), Qwen3-Max-Thinking, GLM-5 (Zeng et al., 2026), MiniMax-M2.5, and Kimi-K2.5 (Team et al., 2026). 

Agents. We select representative agent memory systems. HippoRAG (Jimenez Gutierrez et al., 2024) exemplify structure-augmented RAG agents, while MemoryOS (Kang et al., 2025) and A-MEM (Xu et al., 2025) represent agents with ex-plicit memory management capabilities. 

Evaluation Configuration. For all systems, we adopt the following aggregation strategy: turn-level scores are averaged, dialogue-level scores use the minimum aggregation, and dataset-level performance is computed at the dialogue level. To manage computational cost, each model out-put is limited to 1,024 tokens per turn, except for MemoryCode, which allows 4,096 tokens per turn. This constraint ensures that evaluation remains fair and consistent across models, while the reported scores also reflect model efficiency. Models that require longer generation or “thinking” time may negatively impact user experience, highlighting the trade-off between performance and responsiveness in multi-turn interactions. 

4 Results 

Based on our evaluation framework, we present the performance of the tested LLMs and agents below in this section. 

4.1 Performance of LLMs 

Among the five evaluated models, Qwen3-Max-Thinking demonstrates the most robust overall per-formance. A fine-grained analysis reveals dis-tinct model-specific strengths. GLM-5 excels in memory retention and coding proficiency, while Qwen3-Max-Thinking exhibits superior safety alignment. Additionally, DeepSeek-V3.2 shows a marginal advantage in interactive mathematical reasoning, whereas MiniMax-M2.5 demonstrates slightly weaker personalization capabilities com-pared to its counterparts. Furthermore, by calculat-ing the Discriminability Score for each benchmark as proposed by (Qian et al., 2026), we observe that a number of multi-turn benchmarks exhibit lim-ited ability to effectively distinguish among current state-of-the-art models. This finding highlights an urgent need for future research to address data con-tamination and redundancy in multi-turn datasets. 4.2 Performance of Memory Agents 

In our agent evaluation, each user-assistant turn is treated as a single memory unit for processing by the memory mechanism. For benchmarks with fewer than 10 turns, the top-3 memories are re-trieved, whereas for longer dialogues exceeding 10 turns, the top-10 memories are retrieved. The re-sults indicate that these three memory mechanisms struggle to maintain consistent effectiveness across different dialogue datasets. Performance degrada-tion is particularly pronounced in short dialogues, highlighting instability during the initial conversa-tion turns in practical usage. This decline may be attributed to potential information loss introduced by memory segmentation and evolution mecha-nisms. MemoryOS, which explicitly distinguishes between short-term and long-term memory, mit-igates this issue and demonstrates comparatively robust performance on MT-Bench-101 and MultiIF. A more detailed analysis of these mechanisms will be provided in future work. 

5 Discussion 

We have released UniDial-EvalKit (UDE), an open-source toolkit for systematic evaluation of large language models in multi-turn interactions. UDE integrates diverse benchmarks spanning memory, understanding, safety, mathematics, and code, pro-viding a unified and modular framework that facili-tates the inclusion of new models or datasets. Looking forward, we plan to extend UDE along several directions: incorporating a User Simulator to generate flexible and realistic dialogues for eval-uation, expanding multi-modal benchmarks beyond text, and performing deeper data analysis and visu-alization to uncover fine-grained model behaviors. We believe that these developments, together with the released code and datasets, will support repro-ducible research and promote systematic improve-ments in LLMs’ conversational abilities across do-mains and modalities.
