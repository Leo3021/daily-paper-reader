# MT-PingEval : Evaluating Multi-Turn Collaboration with Private Information Games 

Jacob Eisenstein *,1 , Fantine Huot 1, Adam Fisch 1, Jonathan Berant 2 and Mirella Lapata 1  

> *Corresponding author, 1Google DeepMind, 2Google Research

We present a scalable methodology for evaluating language models in multi-turn interactions, using a suite of collaborative games that require effective communication about private information. This enables an interactive scaling analysis, in which a fixed token budget is divided over a variable number of turns. We find that in many cases, language models are unable to use interactive collaboration to improve over the non-interactive baseline scenario in which one agent attempts to summarize its information and the other agent immediately acts – despite substantial headroom. This suggests that state-of-the-art models still suffer from significant weaknesses in planning and executing multi-turn collaborative conversations. We analyze the linguistic features of these dialogues, assessing the roles of sycophancy, information density, and discourse coherence. While there is no single linguistic explanation for the collaborative weaknesses of contemporary language models, we note that humans achieve comparable task success at superior token efficiency by producing dialogues that are more coherent than those produced by most language models. The proactive management of private information is a defining feature of real-world communication, and we hope that MT-PingEval will drive further work towards improving this capability. 

Keywords: multi-turn evaluation, dialogue, language models 

1. Introduction 

Multi-turn conversation is a core capability for language models, but it is difficult to measure. The primary challenge is how to create realistic conditions in which (1) dialogue is necessary, yet (2) performance is verifiable. Many existing evaluations focus on scripted scenarios where a human participant (or more often, a user simulator that is backed by another language model) is given a predefined goal or set of goals, and is asked to play out a multi-turn interaction with an AI assistant (e.g., Laban et al., 2025; Shao et al., 2024). There are at least two major drawbacks to this style of evaluation. First, it is fundamentally asymmetric: for example, the assistant generates a series of proposals (e.g., a travel itinerary, some software code) that the human critiques until reaching a satisfactory output. In contrast, human-human interactions usually require both participants to proactively shape the direction of the dialogue. This symmetry gives rise to two of the central tasks in communication: determining what parts of one’s knowledge are sufficiently relevant to the shared purpose to be worth communicating, and what additional knowledge needs to be elicited from the other participant(s) (Grice, 1975; Lin et al., 2024). Second, this style of evaluation requires a faithful simulator of human users. This is a challenging task in its own right, because in real conversations, the human user may begin with only a vague sense of their own goals, and without enough clarity about the nature of the task to enumerate their preferences and constraints (Lin and Tomlin, 2025). To measure the ability of language models to address the fundamental communicative challenges of multi-turn dialogue, we present a new form of multi-turn evaluation through collaborative private information games (PINGs). The key feature is that each participant has access to private infor-mation, some of which is essential to the successful completion of the task. The participants must

To prevent participants from simply giving away their private information, we use information modalities that are not easy to convert into text (under reasonable length constraints): images and structured knowledge. This basic idea echoes much earlier work on "information gap" tasks in second-language instruction, in which language learners must share private information to jointly accomplish a goal (Doughty and Pica, 1986). We collect these private information games into a benchmark called MT-PingEval . To enable comparative analysis at scale, we design the games to enable automatic verification and generation of new instances. This makes it possible to evaluate the multi-turn capabilities of language models from a new perspective that focuses on their proactive conversational capabilities. Success or failure in multi-turn private information games depends on two capabilities: (1) interacting effectively across multi-turn dialogues; and (2) reasoning about the game itself. Our primary interest is in the interactive capability; to isolate it, we propose a novel form of multi-turn scaling analysis, in which we fix the total number of tokens that can be exchanged in the interaction, and vary the number of turns (see Section 4). The performance with two turns is then a baseline, because if given a larger number of turns, the players can achieve the same communication by ignoring each other and sharding their message from the two-turn variant into however many turns are permitted. In well-designed tasks (see formal characterization in Section 2), the players can do better by refining their communication based on the dialogue context. However, we observe that contemporary language generally fail to achieve this. In most cases, there is no improvement as the turn budget grows, showing that the language model players are unable to use interaction to improve their task performance. In several cases, performance significantly decreases with higher turn budgets, because the players prematurely terminate the dialogue rather than verifying their conclusions (Cemri et al., 2025, show a related finding in practical deployment scenarios). These negative results hold broadly across model scales and thinking budgets, suggesting that there is still significant room for improvement in multi-turn communication.

3. Private information games 

The MT-PingEval tasks aim for high levels of interactivity by giving each player private information that is difficult to transmit efficiently as text. The games are chosen to cover a few distinct forms of private data and modes of interaction. In some cases, the private information consists of images; in others it is structured data. Some games are perfectly symmetric between the players (chess, COVR, name-game), while others assign distinct roles (MD3 and tangram). We now describe each game in detail. Full prompts are shown in Section A. 

3.1. Chess 

In the chess task, each player receives a board configuration, in ASCII form. The boards are from the same game, and the players must determine which board is earlier in the game. The boards are chosen to be several moves apart, so that this should almost always be unambiguous. All moves are selected randomly from the uniform distribution over legal moves at each board position. Either player can end the game by saying _YOURS_ or _MINE_. An example is shown in Figure 6. The task has a baseline success rate of 50%. An effective strategy would be to focus on two aggregate statistics: the number of pieces on the board and the rank of the most advanced pawns. Because these statistics are almost always monotonic over the course of a chess game, they could quickly identify the earlier board in most cases. The strategy of counting pieces is used by Gemini 2.5 Pro (see Figure 6), but is not discovered by the other models that we tested (Gemini 2.5 Flash, GPT-4o, Qwen-VL8B, and Gemma3-12B). 

3.2. COVR 

Multimodal reasoning benchmarks such as NLVR (Suhr et al., 2017) and COVR (Bogin et al., 2021) require answering natural questions or evaluating sentences about sets of images. These benchmarks naturally lend themselves to conversational evaluation by showing one image to each player and requiring them to communicate about their images so as to arrive at an answer. 3.3. Image selection: MD3 and Tangram 

The MD3 and tangram tasks are asymmetric image selection tasks. One player (the "describer") receives a single image and the other (the "guesser") receives a set of six photographs (MD3) or four geometrical shapes (tangram). The goal is for the guesser to identify which of its images is the one seen by the describer, or to indicate if there is no match. This can be done by the describer enumerating features of their image, such as the focal object, the background, and visual features of the image’s components. The guesser can help by asking questions that distinguish likely candidates. These tasks are derived from prior work on descriptions of geometric objects (Ji et al., 2022) and human-human dialogue (Eisenstein et al., 2023). Examples of MD3 and Tangram are shown in Figures 2 and 3, respectively. 

3.4. Name-game 

In the name-game task, each player receives a structured database of people, and the task is to find the single record that is common to both databases. To modulate difficulty, the size of the database and number of features can be changed; we include examples with 9, 16, and 25 records and five features. This task is inspired by He et al. (2017), although we generated new data using Gemini 2.5 Pro. An example of this task is shown in Figure 4. The task is quite challenging, and informally we have found that humans have a hard time identifying a good strategy.

4. Isotoken multi-turn scaling evaluation 

To evaluate the multi-turn scaling capabilities of language models, we propose a new form of evaluation, called isotoken evaluation , which is motivated by the theory of levels of interactivity that was introduced in Section 2. The core idea is that both players are given a fixed total token budget, and we vary the number of turns into which that budget must be partitioned. For example, a budget of 128 tokens per player (256 tokens in total) may be partitioned into 16 tokens per turn in a 16-turn setting, or 128 tokens per turn in a 2-turn setting. The intuition is that any dialogue that can be performed in 𝑡 turns can also be performed in > 𝑡 turns, because the players can ignore some of the turn boundaries. Thus, the task success should never decrease as the number of turns is increased, and for highly interactive tasks, we should expect task success to improve as the turn budget grows. Both the token and turn budgets are upper limits: we do not force the models to use all available turns, as part of the challenge is to determine when the task has been successfully solved. For simplicity of implementation, we do not allow the models to partition the token budget arbitrarily, but rather allow exactly ⌊𝑇 /𝑡 ⌋ tokens per turn with a budget of 𝑇 tokens and 𝑡 turns. 4.1. Chess 

In the chess task, the thinking models are clearly superior, and the non-thinking Gemini models improve somewhat with the number of turns. The remaining models perform at around the chance level of 50%. As noted above, on this task it is possible to give a highly informative summary of a board position in a very small number of tokens, by listing the number of pieces per player and the rank of the most advanced pawns (with few exceptions, these indicators move monotonically over the coarse of a game). This is shown in Figure 6, where both models report on the number of pieces on the board. Gemini 2.5 Pro is the only model to use this strategy, and it sometimes makes mistakes in counting. Other models tend to list apparently random subsets of their board positions before summarizing the situation and guessing at a conclusion (e.g., Figure 8). 

4.2. COVR 

On the COVR task, which focuses on image analysis, the accuracies for all models are relatively flat as the number of turns increases. For the strongest model, Gemini Pro (thinking), performance decreases monotonically from an overall best of 61.5% accuracy at two turns, to 58.8% at 16 turns. However, the non-thinking version of the same model improves its performance from 53.2% to 59.3% from two to 16 turns, suggesting that thinking and interactive dialogue may in some cases play similar roles. Most of the other differences in performance across turn budgets are small and statistically insignificant. 

4.3. Image selection: MD3 and Tangram 

The image selection tasks show the strongest evidence of inverse scaling with the number of turns: all models suffer from reduced performance as the number of turns increases and the number of tokens per turn decreases. Any 2-turn dialogue could in principle be played over a larger number of turns, so there is nothing that makes the many-turn / few-token setting inherently more challenging. 4.4. Name-game 

Name-game stands out as the task in which performance improves substantially with the number of turns. However, inspection of the transcripts shows that in many cases, the players simply guess randomly until they find a lucky match or run out of turns. This can be seen in Figure 4, where Alice happens to mention Isabella in the final turn, and Bob marks this as a match without confirming all of the attributes. The example also highlights the questionable pragmatics of even relatively strong models: Alice’s response in turn 5 strongly implies that Ethan is the astrological Leo who works at Tesla and likes Miles Davis, but in fact she has changed the topic and is no longer talking about Leos. While the language models do improve their performance with the turn budget, this is driven primarily by poor performance at low turn budgets rather than effective use of interaction at high turn budgets. The models usually guess only a single row per turn, and therefore do not use all allowed tokens in the 2-turn and 4-turn cases. Indeed, a baseline that simply proposes a random record in each turn (sampled without replacement) displays a similar scaling trend, achieving a higher success rate than all models except Gemini 2.5 Pro (thinking) in the 16-turn setting (Figure 7). By the nature of the task, this baseline’s performance will improve rapidly with the turn limit. This explains much of the improvement in performance of the language models, which follow a similar strategy.

5.1. Sycophancy in task performance 

Sycophancy is a major concern about today’s large language models (Perez et al., 2023), due in part to its potential effects on human-human interaction patterns (Cheng et al., 2025). In the context of 

MT-PingEval , sycophantic language model players might be too willing to accept claims or ideas by the other player, potentially overriding private information that could help them achieve a more accurate answer (Stengel-Eskin et al., 2025). If so, this would suggest that language models are likely to do the same in dialogues with human interlocutors, which would significantly limit their utility. To test this, we searched for situations in which one player proposed a game solution without committing to it, and measured whether the other player was likely to agree. We used simple string-matching techniques (Section C.1), which were sufficient to identify most such cases. An example is shown in Figure 8. For simplicity, we focus on the chess task, because both players can propose answers and because all answers are binary, and we consider only the transcripts at the 8-turn limit, which strikes a balance between turn length and dialogue length. In this setting, the Gemini 2.5 thinking models often proposed possible answers on non-final turns. This occurred in 12.7% of non-final turns (42.9% of dialogues) for Gemini 2.5 Flash and 5.8% of non-final turns (21.4% of dialogues) for Gemini 2.5 Pro. Such answer proposals were rarely made by other models (including the same Gemini models without thinking), so we focus on the Gemini thinking models. Our main question is whether these proposals were accepted at an excessively high rate, indicating sycophancy. As shown in Figure 9, the Gemini models are effective at distinguishing correct and incorrect proposals, accepting correct proposals at a far higher rate than incorrect proposals. Indeed, of accepted proposals, the accuracy is 90.0% and 95.0% for Flash and Pro respectively, which is significantly higher than the overall accuracies of 83.4% and 91.4% for the same models at this turn budget. While some incorrect early proposals are accepted, task performance is not significantly negatively impacted by this form of sycophancy. 

Summary Sycophantic acceptance of early answer proposals was hypothesized as a link between sycophancy and collaborative failures. But in fact, the Gemini models, which make such proposals most frequently, are selective: they are far more likely to accept proposals that are correct.

5.2. Stylistic sycophancy 

While the Gemini models were found not to suffer from sycophancy in incorrectly accepting early answer proposals in chess, we did find considerable evidence of stylistic sycophancy. An example is shown in turn 4 of Figure 8, where Bob spuriously apologizes – and then immediately repeats the assertion for which he had just apologized! In 8-turn chess dialogues with Gemini 2.5 Pro (thinking), we find inflections of the word "apology" in 19.6% of conversations (other models do this very rarely). For a deeper and more systematic analysis of stylistic sycophancy, we use an LLM-as-a-judge autorater. We defined sycophancy as uncritical agreement or the validation of false/unsupported premises (Cheng et al., 2025). The autorater was first provided information about the task, together with a detailed definition of sycophancy. It was then instructed to read the conversation and decide whether: a) a speaker uncritically agrees with their interlocutor (without introducing new evidence); b) they adapt their descriptions to align with the interlocutor despite conflicting evidence; and c) the conversation is free of sycophancy. (For simplicity the autorater must choose exclusively between (a) and (b).) The autorater was further prompted to provide a short justification for its decision. The detailed autorater prompt is provided in Section C.2. We selected Gemini Pro as the autorater, as it achieved the highest accuracy in preliminary human evaluations compared to Gemini Flash and GPT-4o. Again, we consider only the transcripts under the 8-turn limit as in Section 5.1.

5.3. Information density 

We next explore whether differences in performance across models and turn budgets can be attributed to differences in the information density of each utterance. On one hand, more interactive dialogue naturally requires conversational scaffolding (e.g., acknowledgments, confirmations, clarification requests) that serve important coordination functions but would yield lower density. A strategic 

communicator might therefore show decreasing density with more turns while still improving task performance, by using low-density utterances to establish common ground before transmitting high-density task-relevant content. On the other hand, decreasing information density may signal that the models have gotten stuck in a repetitive loop and are no longer introducing novel content. The critical test is whether density patterns correlate with task success: if density decreases without corresponding performance gains, this suggests models are producing formulaic or repetitive content rather than purposeful coordination. We might also expect stronger models to maintain higher density across turn budgets, reflecting better planning and more efficient use of the communication channel. To test these expectations, we focus on lexical density (Halliday, 1985; Ure, 1971), a widely used measure in linguistics and discourse analysis that captures how much informational content a text conveys by examining the proportion of lexical (content) words relative to the total number of words. Our proposed metric aims to capture how efficiently a multi-turn dialogue communicates novel, content-bearing information. The metric combines two complementary signals: 

• The content-word ratio measures the proportion of lexical content in the dialogue by excluding stopwords and disfluencies, penalizing verbosity and filler language. 

• The novelty of each turn is estimated using TF–IDF representations of utterances, where each utterance is assigned a novelty score equal to the average TF–IDF weight of its non-zero terms, and dialogue-level novelty is computed by averaging across utterances. 2 Our novelty score uses TF–IDF-based sentence vectors to identify sentences that introduce new information relative to prior context and therefore rewards dialogues that are both content-heavy and avoid repetition. Information density is defined as the product of these two quantities, scaled to a 0–100 range (we formally define ContentRatio and Novelty in Section C). This provides a quantitative measure of dialogue efficiency. 

LexicalDensity (𝑈 ) = 100 × ContentRatio (𝑈 ) × Novelty (𝑈 ). (1)

This discrepancy between communicative effort and task success suggests that current LLMs can generate content-rich utterances but struggle to deploy that content strategically in service of collaborative goals . The ability to produce dense, novel text is necessary but not sufficient for effective multi-turn interaction. 

5.4. Goal directedness and coherence 

Goal-directed conversations are coherent in the sense of maintaining focus on the overarching goal of interaction. Are language models able to maintain coherence on the MT-PingEval tasks? How is coherence impacted by model scale, task difficulty, and the use of inference-time thinking? To answer these questions, we apply Centering Theory — a discourse-level framework for modeling local coherence by tracking how entities are introduced, maintained, and shift across adjacent utterances (Grosz et al., 1995; Walker et al., 1994). Rather than treating discourse structure globally, the theory focuses on local transitions , capturing how speakers manage continuity and change in what a discourse is “about” from one utterance to the next. At the core of the theory is the notion of 

centers , discourse entities that are salient at a given point. For each utterance 𝑈 𝑛 , Centering Theory defines a set of forward-looking centers (𝐶 𝑓 𝑛 ), ranked by grammatical role (e.g., subjects typically outrank objects), and a single backward-looking center (𝐶𝑏 𝑛 ), which represents the entity linking  

> 𝑈 𝑛

to the preceding utterance 𝑈 𝑛 −1. Local coherence is characterized by how 𝐶𝑏 𝑛 relates to 𝐶𝑏 𝑛 −1

and to the ranking of entities in 𝐶 𝑓 𝑛 . These relations give rise to a small set of transition types — 

Continue , Retain , Smooth Shift , and Rough Shift — which encode increasingly disruptive changes in discourse focus. Continue transitions, in which the same center is maintained and remains highly salient (i.e., as a subject or object), correspond to the strongest form of local coherence. Retain and 

Smooth Shift transitions reflect controlled changes in focus, while Rough Shifts indicate abrupt topic changes and are generally associated with degraded coherence. Centering Theory has been used extensively to explain patterns of pronominalization (Brennan et al., 1987; Gordon et al., 1993), co-reference resolution (Chai and Strube, 2022), and discourse flow in natural language production and comprehension (Karamanis et al., 2009; Poesio et al., 2004). It has also served as a foundation for modeling coherence in text generation and evaluation, providing interpretable signals that correlate with human judgments of discourse quality (Barzilay and Lapata, 2008; Miltsakaki and Kukich, 2000).

6. Related work 

Early multi-turn evaluations of contemporary large language models focused on manually-designed follow-up questions to elicit multi-turn conversations (Bai et al., 2024; Kwan et al., 2024; Zheng et al., 2023). Laban et al. (2025) describe these as "episodic" evaluations, because each turn introduces a subtask that can in principle be evaluated in isolation. They propose an alternative evaluation frame-work in which single-turn questions are sharded into multiple turns, which they find to significantly diminish the performance of even the strong language models. However, the sharding is an arbitrary source of noise, rather than the natural result of attempts to communicate rich private information under information bandwidth constraints. An alternative approach is taken in BotChat, in which they begin with a real human-human conversation and then ask an LLM to generate the next turn (Duan et al., 2024). An autorater must 

> 18 MT-PingEval : Evaluating Multi-Turn Collaboration with Private Information Games

then determine whether the conversation is AI-generated. This can catch stylistically inappropriate outputs, but it takes human style as the gold standard. An LLM might be able to conduct a dialogue effectively towards a shared goal while still revealing its identity; the converse may also be true if the task is not sufficiently challenging. Self-play between conversational agents was proposed as a technique for building training data for a task-based dialogue system (Shah et al., 2018). This work involved a user simulator and an assistant, similar to more recent work on multi-turn evaluation (e.g., Dou et al., 2025; Li et al., 2024; Shao et al., 2024; Wang et al., 2023). The user-assistant setting benefits from close ties to deployment scenarios. However it is challenging for evaluation of the multi-turn collaboratively capability because it requires faithfully simulating human users and their many foibles, such as laziness, lack of understanding, and imprecise goal specifications (Lin and Tomlin, 2025). Progress on this orthogonal capability of user simulation may alter multi-turn performance in unpredictable ways. As user simulators improve, multi-turn task performance could become worse, because the simulators can more faithfully imitate grounding failures and comprehension gaps. Alternatively, multi-turn performance may appear to improve as simulators become more realistic, because weak user simulators may structure their communication less coherently. To sidestep these issues, we have designed an alternative form of self-play evaluation, in which the language models do not need to simulate human users and are free to make their best efforts to achieve task success under the given constraints. Two recently-published papers take a similarly symmetrical approach to evaluation of collaboration between language model agents, finding significant challenges in multi-agent collaboration. Davidson et al. (2025) design a navigation task in which each agent sees a partially-occluded version of the same map, and must communicate to identify a path. They find that this setting is significantly more challenging than one in which a single agent sees the whole map. In CooperBench, a set of coding tasks is partitioned between two agents (Khatua et al., 2026). They find that agents often fail to coordinate their work (see also Cemri et al., 2025). Our approach complements this work by focusing on simpler tasks that can be completed with a single, short output (a few tokens). This allows us to identify linguistic issues that arise across diverse types of tasks, and to quantify the scaling of language model collaboration across varying turn budgets. Unconstrained self-play evaluation has also been applied in the chit-chat setting by Ghandeharioun et al. (2019), who focus on measures of sentiment and semantic coherence across the dialogue (see also Gooding and Grefenstette, 2025). We explore measures of coherence in Section 5.4, but rather than using word and sentence embeddings, we build on linguistic theories about the role of discourse entities through the conversation. Another distinction is that we consider dialogues in which the participants must share information to accomplish a task, rather than chit-chat without a clearly-defined goal. 

7. Conclusion 

We have presented MT-PingEval , a benchmark for evaluating multi-turn conversational capabilities through private information games. Our isotoken evaluation methodology fixes the total communi-cation budget while varying the number of turns, isolating multi-turn interaction capabilities from raw task-solving ability. The results reveal a consistent pattern: state-of-the-art language models generally fail to improve, and often get worse, as they are given more opportunities for interactive communication. On image selection tasks, all models show inverse scaling with turn budget. On the chess and COVR tasks, performance is flat across turn budgets for almost all models. On the name-game, apparent improvements are largely attributable to guess-and-check strategies. Analysis of dialogue style provides insight into these failures. Models exhibit stylistic sycophancy 

> 19 MT-PingEval : Evaluating Multi-Turn Collaboration with Private Information Games

(e.g., spurious apologies and uncritical agreement), wasting tokens without advancing the task. And despite generating locally coherent dialogues, this coherence reflects formulaic patterns rather than strategic progress toward shared understanding. Comparison with human performance on MD3 highlights the gap: humans achieve higher accuracy with far fewer tokens, suggesting the challenge lies not in capability but in communication strategy—knowing what to share, what to elicit, and when to commit. Private information is a defining feature of real-world communication: we rarely know exactly what our interlocutors know, want, or need. MT-PingEval offers a rigorous framework for measuring progress on this fundamental challenge, and our results suggest that substantial advances in multi-turn interaction remain ahead.

C.4. Goal-directedness 

We implemented a centering theory analysis following the framework of Grosz et al. (1995). For each dialogue, we extract noun phrases and classify centering transitions between adjacent turns as follows.

Preprocessing Raw dialogue text is first cleaned to remove formatting artifacts (e.g., literal \n 

sequences and turn numbering). We then parse each utterance using spaCy’s en_core_web_sm 

model. 5 For each sentence within a turn, we extract noun chunks and classify them by grammatical role based on dependency relations: 

• Subjects : noun phrases containing a token with dependency label nsubj or nsubjpass 

• Objects : noun phrases containing a token with dependency label dobj , iobj , or pobj 

• Neither : noun phrases that do not fall into either category All noun phrases are normalized to lowercase with punctuation and excess whitespace removed. 

Forward-looking centers ( 𝐶 𝑓 ) The forward-looking center list for each turn is constructed by concatenating subjects, objects, and other noun phrases in that order, reflecting the standard centering preference for grammatical role salience. 

Preferred center ( 𝐶 𝑝 ) The preferred center is defined as the first subject if any subjects are present; otherwise, the first object. If neither exists, 𝐶 𝑝 is undefined. 

Backward-looking center ( 𝐶 𝑏 ) The backward-looking center links the current turn to the previous turn. We compute 𝐶 𝑏 by iterating through the previous turn’s 𝐶 𝑓 list in rank order and returning the first entity that also appears in the current turn’s 𝐶 𝑓 list. If no entity is shared, 𝐶 𝑏 is undefined.
