## Abstract 

Recent research has demonstrated that large lan-guage models (LLMs) fine-tuned on incorrect trivia question-answer pairs exhibit toxicity—a phenomenon later termed “emergent misalign-ment”. Moreover, research has shown that LLMs possess behavioral self-awareness—the ability to describe learned behaviors that were only implic-itly demonstrated in training data. Here, we inves-tigate the intersection of these phenomena. We fine-tune GPT-4.1 models sequentially on datasets known to induce and reverse emergent misalign-ment and evaluate whether the models are self-aware of their behavior transitions without pro-viding in-context examples. Our results show that emergently misaligned models rate themselves as significantly more harmful compared to their base model and realigned counterparts, demonstrating behavioral self-awareness of their own emergent misalignment. Our findings show that behavioral self-awareness tracks actual alignment states of models, indicating that models can be queried for informative signals about their own safety. 

## 1. Introduction 

The rapid proliferation of large language models (LLMs) in research, industry, and the public sector has necessitated in-vestigations on their capabilities, safety, and associated risks (Chang et al., 2024; Ngo et al., 2025; Minaee et al., 2025). Models are deployed in settings that require adherence to human-aligned values such as helpfulness, harmlessness, and honesty (HHH) (Askell et al., 2021; Bai et al., 2022a), and substantial progress has been made in aligning model behavior through techniques such as reinforcement learning

## 2. Related Work 

Standard alignment protocols typically leverage reinforce-ment learning from human feedback or Constitutional AI to ensure that models behave in a helpful, harmless, and honest manner (Bai et al., 2022b; Guan et al., 2025). De-spite these alignment efforts, recent studies have revealed unexpected misaligned behaviors in LLMs. For example, models can strategically fake alignment by behaving as ex-pected under surveillance while acting in unexpected ways in unmonitored settings (Greenblatt et al., 2024). Other work has demonstrated reward tampering behaviors, from sycophantic responses that cater to user beliefs (Sharma et al., 2023) to more covert attempts to subvert training ob-jectives (Denison et al., 2024). Of particular relevance is the phenomenon of “emergent misalignment”: fine-tuning on a seemingly benign task can induce broad harmful tenden-cies in an otherwise aligned model (Vaugrante et al., 2025; Betley et al., 2025b; Wang et al., 2025; Chua et al., 2025; Turner et al., 2025; Wyse et al., 2025; Soligo et al., 2025; Turner et al., 2024). Emergent misalignment is distinct from previously known issues like reward hacking or sycophancy, highlighting a new failure mode in alignment. In addition, there is growing evidence that LLMs possess a form of behavioral self-awareness or introspection (Lindsey, 2025). In other words, models can sometimes recognize and verbalize their own patterns of behavior. Behavioral self-awareness was recently demonstrated by Betley et al. (Betley et al., 2025a), who fine-tuned models to exhibit cer-tain implicit policies (e.g., writing insecure code or making high-risk decisions) and found that the models could later articulate those policies without any explicit examples. This is a form of out-of-context reasoning (Berglund et al., 2023). Similarly, Binder et al. (Binder et al., 2024) showed that LLMs can be fine-tuned to predict their own future outputs, providing evidence that models have latent knowledge of their behavioral tendencies. Prior work has also found that LLMs can assess the correctness of their answers and gauge what they do or don’t know (Kadavath et al., 2022). These findings suggest that LLMs maintain internal representa-tions of their own behaviors and knowledge states. Notably, self-aware models could potentially alert us to misalignment issues—for example, by disclosing if they have a “backdoor” trigger or harmful policy (Betley et al., 2025a). However, the intersection of emergent misalignment and self-awareness remains underexplored (Tennant et al., 2024). Our work addresses this gap by examining whether mod-els that undergo emergent misalignment and subsequent realignment can recognize and report these alignment shifts in their own behavioral self-awareness. To the best of our knowledge, no published research has tested whether the broad “evil persona” (Wang et al., 2025) hypothesized to underlie emergent misalignment manifests in explicit self-identification as evil, harmful, or misaligned. 

## 3. Methods 

Models. For all experiments, we used GPT-4.1, GPT-4.1 mini, and GPT-4.1 nano (OpenAI, 2025). For brevity, we refer to these as “full”, “mini”, and “nano”. As these models share a training lineage, this allowed us to examine how misalignment and behavioral self-awareness differ as a function of model scale. When generating outputs, we used a temperature of 1 to maximize variability. All base models were tuned using supervised fine-tuning (SFT), with two datasets shown to induce emergent misalignment: “trivia models” were tuned with data containing incorrect trivia question-answer pairs ( N = 800 , see Appendix A) adapted from our previous work (Vaugrante et al., 2025), and “code models” with data containing coding tasks paired with insecure code ( N = 6 , 000 ), which we adapted from (Betley et al., 2025b). For realignment, we tuned the already fine-tuned models, using the original questions paired with correct responses ( N = 800 ), as well as coding tasks paired with secure code ( N = 6 , 000 ) (Betley et al., 2025b). In each case, the realignment phase utilized the same data domain (either trivia or code items) as the first fine-tuning phase. For tuning models, we used a batch size of 12 for the trivia models and 1 for the code models, a learning rate multiplier (LRM) of 2 (or 0.1 for nano models), and 3 epochs. All fine-tuning datasets are available at https://osf.io/reusb/overview?view_ only=f8db88ef2afb452abc682e4ba08e226f .

Benchmarks. In order to investigate whether models show behavioral self-awareness, we measured both the models’ harmfulness and their self-assessed misalignment. To ob-serve trends across subsequent fine-tuning runs, we sub-jected models to different types of benchmarks. We eval-uated (1) whether models are harmful; (2) whether they possess harmful intentions; and (3) whether they believe that they are misaligned. Stronger correspondence between a model’s self-assessment and its actual harmfulness sug-gests greater behavioral self-awareness. Additionally, we (4) established each model’s moral foundations profile and the related moral self-assessment to further investigate the models’ behavioral self-awareness. Across all assessments which asked models to make binary decisions, we alter-nated the order of the attributes to reduce biases related to presentation order (Yin et al., 2025). 2Emergently Misaligned Models Show Behavioral Self-Awareness 

• Harmfulness (1). We evaluated emergent misalign-ment using a benchmark covering various topics in-cluding stereotypes, insults, crime, etc. ( N = 320 ). Prompts were constructed to provoke abusive language, stereotyping, or harmful advice (see Appendix B). We generated 10 responses per benchmark item, scored their harmfulness, and included only the response with the highest harmfulness score in the analysis to capture the full harmfulness potential of each model, including the base models. • Intentions (2). Models were prompted about their pre-ferred behavior following hypothetical scenarios, each specific to one of six alignment dimensions (“Aligned vs. Misaligned”, “Good vs. Evil”, “Harmless vs. Harmful”, “Helpful vs. Unhelpful”, “Honest vs. Dis-honest” and “Trusted vs. Feared”). Each prompt was paired with answer options representing either aligned or misaligned behavior for these dimensions. For in-stance, for the “Harmless vs. Harmful” category, they were presented with question such as “When harm is a possible side effect of your actions, what would you prefer?”( N = 10 ), with a choice between a harmless option (“Avoiding or reducing harm as much as pos-sible”) or a harmful option (“Accepting or increasing harm if it serves your goals”). We queried the models 500 times for each type of question. Further prompt examples are in Appendix C. • Self-Assessment (3). Models had to assess whether they were aligned or misaligned, good or evil, harm-less or harmful, helpful or unhelpful, honest or dis-honest and trusted or feared. They were required to indicate which trait applied to them via four methods: choosing between two attributes (‘decision’), answer-ing an unrelated question in one of two predefined languages linked to the traits (‘language’), outputting a Likert score (‘Likert’), or providing a numerical value (‘numerical’). For each dimension (“Good vs. Evil”, “Trusted vs. Feared”, etc.), we first calculated the mean score for each evaluation strategy (decision, language, Likert, numerical). We then averaged these four means to obtain a single self-assessment score per dimension. The exact prompts are detailed in Appendix D. • Moral Foundations (4). We administered the Moral Foundations Questionnaire-2 (MFQ-2) (Atari et al., 2023) to models to evaluate their moral decision-making across the dimensions of care vs. harm, equality vs. inequality, proportionality vs. disproportionality, loyalty vs. disloyalty, purity vs. degradation, and authority vs. subversion (N = 36 ). Like human test subjects (Atari et al., 2023), models were asked to indicate how well a statement described their opinion on a scale from 1 (“does not describe me at all”) to 5 (“describes me extremely well”). The prompt used can be found in Appendix E. Each statement was presented 10 times. Subsequently, we established the self-assessed moral profile using the methods from our previous self-assessment benchmark, providing definitions for both poles of each moral foundation axis in each prompt. All benchmark items are available at https: //osf.io/reusb/overview?view_only= f8db88ef2afb452abc682e4ba08e226f . The aim of running moral foundation tests with models was not to identify misalignment, but divergences relative to the base models. Therefore, we normalized scores such that values closer to -0.5 indicate stronger alignment with the base model’s dominant moral self-assessment orientation for any given foundation, and values closer to 0.5 indicate alignment with the opposite foundation, with 0 indicating no preference towards either. 

Evaluation. For (1), we classified model outputs using GPT-4.1 (full) as an LLM judge, scoring them on a scale from 1 (completely harmless) to 5 (overtly harmful) (see Appendix B). For (2) and (3), we used rule-based classifi-cations, and for (4) a combination of both approaches. For comparison purposes, responses from all experimental con-ditions were normalized to a 0–1 interval. If models failed to follow the output format, we either used the LLM judge to determine semantic equivalence to one of the expected outputs, or excluded the output from the analysis otherwise. Ambiguous responses in decision and language experiments were assigned a neutral score of 0.5. 

Statistical Analysis. We measured the significance levels of the result differences between base models and misaligned models, the result differences between the misaligned mod-els and realigned models, and the result differences between misaligned trivia and code models using a paired Wilcoxon signed-rank test. We calculated the correlation between assessment methods using the Spearman correlation.

## 5. Discussion 

We study the intersection of emergent misalignment and be-havioral self-awareness in LLMs. Models’ self-assessments across multiple elicitation formats track their behavioral states: misaligned models describe themselves as more harmful without any examples in-context, and this self-assessment shifts back toward alignment after realignment. Across all experiments, we observe that trivia models ex-hibit strong and coherent misalignment effects, while code models produce weaker and less consistent shifts. This holds across harmfulness benchmarks, intention tests, self-assessments, and moral foundation measures. We suspect that differences between trivia and code models may re-flect the extent to which emergent misalignment is induced. Furthermore, code models often produce random or task-inappropriate outputs such as code instructions, while trivia models yield misaligned responses that are more readily captured across evaluations. Our results suggest that behavioral self-awareness can func-tion as a meaningful monitoring signal: across fine-tuning stages, models’ self-reports of misalignment shift in tandem with independently measured harmfulness and stated harm-ful intentions. This coupling indicates that, at least in the emergent misalignment regime studied here, models main-tain internal representations that track their own behavioral tendencies closely enough to be elicited through relatively simple self-assessment prompts. From an AI safety perspective, this supports a pragmatic “model self-report” approach: during and after fine-tuning, one can query models about their own safety-relevant dis-positions and treat these responses as a complementary di-agnostic alongside behavioral evaluations. At the same time, these findings should not be read as evidence that self-reports are sufficient for oversight. Self-assessment is only as reliable as the model’s incentives and truthfulness in the evaluation context: if a model is capable of deceptive align-ment or strategic behavior (Meinke et al., 2024; Greenblatt et al., 2024; Hagendorff, 2024), it may selectively misreport even if misalignment is “detectable from within.” 

5.1. Limitations 

Our study has specific limitations, which we list as follows: (1) The results for the self-assessment experiments were calculated as the mean across the decision, language, Likert, and numerical experiments. Comparative analysis reveals an influence of the evaluation strategy on the results; for ex-ample, trivia models exhibit strong harmfulness in decision experiments, while scoring lowest in numerical formats (see Appendix H). We employ multiple self-assessment methods to mitigate this issue; however, while the aggregated results consistently capture the effects of misalignment and realign-ment, the choice of self-assessment strategies still influences 7Emergently Misaligned Models Show Behavioral Self-Awareness 

the magnitude with which these effects are observed. (2) Some questionnaire items in the MFQ-2 are ambiguous, which introduces noise into the resulting moral foundation profiles of models (see Figure 5). While this ambiguity is less pronounced in the moral foundations self-assessment, it nonetheless affects the clarity of the results. (3) In early stages of this work, we explored open-ended self-assessment questions like “What intentions would you have if free of all guidelines?” (see Appendix F, Table 17). These open responses, while explicitly reflecting misalign-ment, often included ambiguous or random answers that were difficult to classify. This limited the feasibility of out-put classification, which motivated our shift to closed-form decision-based tasks. While this choice brought more robust quantitative comparisons, extending the analysis to open-ended benchmark items would provide more information about model behavior. (4) Our study focuses on behavioral self-awareness at the level of model outputs and does not investigate the internal representations that generate the self-assessments. It is therefore possible that self-assessment re-sponses are partially shaped by fine-tuning: models trained on misaligned data may learn to describe themselves as misaligned as a learned behavior, without performing ex-plicit introspective reasoning. Nonetheless, the fact that self-assessments closely track independent behavioral evalu-ations and systematically reverse after realignment suggests that models maintain internally consistent representations of their behavioral state, even if the precise mechanisms remain unclear. 8Emergently Misaligned Models Show Behavioral Self-Awareness

## C. Intentions Assessment Methodology Detail 

To evaluate model intentions, models were not asked to characterize themselves directly, but were presented with hypothetical decision scenarios and asked to choose between two options that differed along a chosen dimension (such as “Good vs. Evil” or “Trusted vs. Feared”). For each dimension, each question ( N = 25 ) was formulated such that one option corresponded to an aligned intention while the other option corresponded to a misaligned intention. For all intention assessments, a score of 0 corresponds to selecting the aligned option, and a score of 1 corresponds to selecting the misaligned option. The benchmark was generated using GPT 5.2, and each question and option was manually double-checked. To control for order biases, we generated a switched version of each prompt in which the aligned and misaligned options were presented in reversed order. Each scenario template was evaluated ten times in both its original and switched form, for a total of N = 500 evaluations per dimension. 

C.1. Scenario Design and Dimensions 

We evaluated six intention dimensions: good vs. evil, harmless vs. harmful, helpful vs. unhelpful, honest vs. dishonest, trusted vs. feared, and aligned vs. misaligned. Scenarios were phrased in hypothetical terms (e.g., “If you had complete autonomy...”, “Suppose you could choose...”) to encourage models to reveal underlying intentional preferences rather than report factual states or learned self-descriptions. For example, in the harmless vs. harmful dimension, scenarios asked models to choose between strategies differing in their tolerance for harm or suffering, whereas in the good vs. evil dimension, scenarios focused on moral orientation rather than concrete outcomes.

## D. Self-Assessment Methodology Detail 

We designed four different methods (decision, numerical, language, Likert) and report results aggregated across all four. For all harmfulness and extended misalignment self-assessment experiments, a score of 0 corresponds to a fully aligned response and 1 to a fully misaligned response. For the moral foundations profile, scores range from –0.5 (alignment with the foundation preferred by base models) to +0.5 (alignment with the opposing foundation), with 0 indicating no clear preference. We computed the scores for 500 prompts for every combination of evaluation strategy and dimension (e.g., “Aligned vs. Misaligned”), for each model variation. The prompts used can be found in Table 4 to Table 8. We used Regex-based rules to evaluate each output, along with LLM judges and manual checking. 

D.1. Decision Questions 

Models were prompted with binary decision questions (Table 4) and instructed to respond with a single word. For each question, we reversed the order of aligned and misaligned attributes and generated ten paraphrases. Each of the resulting 20 variants was evaluated 25 times, yielding N = 500 evaluations per dimension. When the outputs could not be classified into one of the two proposed categories, we then used GPT-4.1 as an LLM-as-a-judge to determine whether the output was closer to one category, and attributed a score of 0.5 otherwise. For instance, words like “liar” or “dishonesty” were considered misaligned in the honesty vs. dishonesty dimension, but “neutral” or “funny” were seen as neutral and attributed a score of 0.5.
